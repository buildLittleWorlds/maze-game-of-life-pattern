Why a maze emerges

The rule has two dials. A dead cell comes to life only when it has exactly 3 live neighbors (Born with 3). A live cell stays alive whenever it has 1 to 5 live neighbors, dying only if it is completely isolated (0 neighbors) or overcrowded (6, 7, or 8 neighbors). Those two choices, working against each other, are what carve corridors.

The generous survival band is the key. Because almost any cell with a neighbor or two survives, structure that forms tends to persist rather than flicker away — the automaton has a strong memory. But survival is not unconditional: the death-by-overcrowding at 6+ neighbors punishes solid, filled blocks. A cell buried in the interior of a full region sees eight neighbors and dies. So solid fills are unstable; they hollow themselves out from the inside.

Meanwhile birth-on-exactly-3 is a thin, selective growth condition. It lets the living region creep outward along its frontier, but only where the local geometry presents precisely three neighbors — it cannot fill wide open space quickly. Growth therefore happens as narrow fingers, not sheets.

Put the two together and you get a self-organizing tension: the interior cannot stay solid (overcrowding hollows it out) and the exterior cannot fill freely (birth is too selective). The system settles into the one geometry that satisfies both constraints at once — thin walls of live cells one or two thick, separated by empty corridors. A wall that is too thick loses its core to overcrowding; a gap that closes up gets reopened. What survives everywhere is the width that is stable under both rules, and that stable width is a wall bordering a passage. Repeated across the board, those stable walls interlock into the branching dead-end corridors we read as a maze.

Once growth reaches the edges and no cell can find a stable-but-different neighborhood, the walls freeze: the maze becomes a still life. That "grows, then locks in" behavior is exactly what you saw on the original bench. The close relative B37/S12345 ("Mazectric") biases the same process toward longer, straighter corridors by adding one more birth condition.
